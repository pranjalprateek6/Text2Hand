Zara woke before dawn and put the kettle on. The old lighthouse hummed
quietly above the bay, and the wind pushed against every window.
Six gulls drifted past the glass. She counted them twice.

Jasper, her uncle, kept a journal on the desk by the stairs. Every
evening he wrote a few lines about the weather, the boats, and the
strange yellow fog that arrived each October.

Quiet mornings require patience, he had written. Nothing exact ever
happens here. A box of maps sat beneath the window, unopened since
July. Zara unfolded one and traced the coast with her thumb.

Kites, xylophones, zebras: the village school had an odd list of
things to bring. Vivian, who taught there, would quiz the children
on Friday about oxen, jazz, and the major rivers of the north.

Grey light arrived slowly. Under the fog the harbour looked calm,
though seven fishing boats had already gone out. Zara walked down,
zipped her jacket, and waved. Nobody waved back yet.

"Are you the keeper now?" asked a boy holding a bucket of crabs.
She laughed. Perhaps! Nobody had told her, but somebody must climb
the stairs each evening and switch the great lamp on.

Xavier, the harbour master, enjoyed music and kept a small radio
humming in his hut. He said the light was pure magic, and that Dawn
was the only honest hour. Marta, his wife, simply laughed at him.

Later she found a key (small, brass, very worn) taped beneath the
third step; it opened a cupboard full of charts from 1908. Numbers
crowded every margin: 2, 3, 45, 67, and a faded 0.

Bells rang at noon. Rain followed. When the lamp turned, the light
swept the water and the whole bay went quiet. Yes, Zara wrote,
today I began. Cold hands, warm tea, and traffic on the far road.
